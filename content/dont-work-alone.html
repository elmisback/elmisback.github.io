Nov 29. For the next month, I’ll be building a portfolio of work that other people might care about. I’m starting today with Blog Post Number One, Don’t Work Alone. I think it’s a mess, but I’d love to hear that confirmed. If you have thoughts about my work, please send them to @elmisback on twitter or to elmisback at gmail.

For 8 months, I searched by myself for what to do with my life. It was very hard and it didn’t amount to much.

don't work alone.

I wanted a job I’d like. The people I admire have jobs they like, and they usually make their own jobs. So I decided it was time for me to make my own job, too.

I had already looked at the existing options–full-time positions at companies that do good work. Still, my spirit balked at the idea of working for someone else. I’m a stubborn person; it made sense to try working alone. I am in my early twenties, and I had enough money saved from a previous job to do this.

What I tried, and how I failed. First, I tried being a writer, because I’ve always wanted to be a writer. If you want to improve your writing, everyone says the same thing: you have to write a lot and get feedback on your writing. I did write a lot, but I didn’t show it to anyone, so I didn’t improve. (I didn’t show it to anyone because it was bad. I can see the irony, too.) Not improving is no fun, so I stopped writing.

After that, I tried developing a scheduling app that acted like a personal planner—it would juggle your schedule if you asked it to, or predict whether you’d be able to manage everything you said you wanted to do today. Unfortunately, I didn’t have enough experience to implement my ideas. I tried for several weeks to make the app work anyway and ended up feeling very dumb and discouraged. I solved the main issues, but left myself burnt out, and abandoned the project as soon as I hit another bug.1

Most recently, I began tutoring students in math and physics, hoping to convince them to use a spaced repetition software and gather data on its impact on their performance. I put together a pitch, set up accounts and decks and a tutorial, but I couldn’t convince the students to use the software, so I’ve given up on introducing it.

Reflection. I invested months of work into each of these efforts (and a few others), and then, every time, I stopped. Each endeavor survived many near-givings-up before the final one, and the final concession was only special in that it was final. In every case, there were still ways to continue—they’re always there—but I gave up anyway.

I have an average-at-worst amount of willpower: I exercise regularly, always cook for myself, and play an instrument (piano, very poorly). I don’t think I give up on things anomalously quickly.2 The only significant difference between me and the average person was that, in these situations, I was much more alone. No one else depended on my work’s success—not even the people I thought it would help. When I stopped caring about a project, no one cared at all, and it died like an unwatered plant.

Persisting in doing hard stuff alone is stupidly hard. If you are doing it, stop. Solve other people’s problems. Come back to your own work when you have more experience—when it’s “easier”—and you have an audience that understands how your work is their problem. If you’re a writer who wants to improve, you can get experience fast by writing stuff that people will actually, for-real read. If you’re an inventor or a developer, you need to show people you have good ideas by building things they want. “Showing off” like this is more important to your growth than knowing what you want to build for yourself.3 4

To be clear, I’m not claiming you can’t run a one-man business. I’m claiming that a one-man business with no clients (and calling itself its own client?) will eventually collapse. Clients are part of a business. Other people are necessary to whatever I’m going to work on while I’m in the world, so it’s sensible to pay attention to them and what they want. I am going nowhere without them.
