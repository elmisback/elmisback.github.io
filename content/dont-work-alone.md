%Don't Work Alone
%Edward Misback
%11/29/2017

For 8 months, I searched by myself for what to do with my life. It was very
hard and it didn't amount to much.

---

**`don't work alone.`**  

---

I wanted a job I'd like. The people I admire have jobs they like, and they
usually make their own jobs. So I decided it was time for me to make my own
job, too.

I had already looked at the existing options--full-time positions at companies
that do good work. Still, my spirit balked at the idea of working for someone
else. I'm a stubborn person; it made sense to try working alone. I am in my
early twenties, and I had enough money saved from a previous job to do this.

**What I tried, and how I failed.**
First, I tried being a writer, because I've always wanted to be a writer. If
you want to improve your writing, everyone says the same thing: you have to
write a lot and get feedback on your writing. I did write a lot, but I didn't
show it to anyone, so I didn't improve. (I didn't show it to anyone because it
was bad. I can see the irony, too.) Not improving is no fun, so I stopped
writing.

After that, I tried developing a scheduling app that acted like a personal
planner---it would juggle your schedule if you asked it to, or predict whether
you'd be able to manage everything you said you wanted to do today.
Unfortunately, I didn't have enough experience to implement my ideas. I tried
for several weeks to make the app work anyway and ended up feeling very dumb
and discouraged. I solved the main issues, but left myself burnt out, and
abandoned the project as soon as I hit another bug.[^1]

Most recently, I began tutoring students in math and physics, hoping to
convince them to use a [spaced
repetition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spaced_repetition)
[software](https://apps.ankiweb.net/) and gather data on its impact on their
performance. I put together a pitch, set up accounts and decks and a tutorial,
but I couldn't convince the students to use the software, so I've given up on
introducing it.

**Reflection.**
I invested months of work into each of these efforts (and a few others), and
then, every time, I stopped. Each endeavor survived many near-givings-up before
the final one, and the final concession was only special in that it was final.
In every case, there were still ways to continue---they're always there---but I
gave up anyway.

I have an average-at-worst amount of willpower: I exercise regularly, always
cook for myself, and play an instrument (piano, very poorly). I don't think I
give up on things anomalously quickly.[^2] The only significant difference
between me and the average person was that, in these situations, I was *much
more alone*.  No one else depended on my work's success---not even the people I
thought it would help. When I stopped caring about a project, no one cared at
all, and it died like an unwatered plant.

**Persisting in doing hard stuff alone is stupidly hard.** If you are doing it,
stop. Solve other people's problems. Come back to your own work when you have
more experience---when it's "easier"---and you have an audience that understands
how your work is their problem. If you're a writer who wants to improve, you
can get experience fast by writing stuff that people will actually, for-real
read. If you're an inventor or a developer, you need to show people you have
good ideas by building things they want. "Showing off" like this is more
important to your growth than knowing what you want to build for
yourself.[^3] [^4] 

To be clear, I'm not claiming you can't run a one-man business. I'm claiming
that a one-man business *with no clients* (and calling itself its own client?)
will eventually collapse.  Clients are part of a business. Other people are
necessary to whatever I'm going to work on while I'm in the world, so it's
sensible to pay attention to them and what they want. I am going nowhere
without them.

[^1]: I still think this app is a good idea---it's a step beyond the pen-and-paper
schedulers that most scheduling apps model. The features I wanted were oddballs
(eg. local notifications instead of push, draggable HTML elements) sprinkled
across a gallimaufry of incompatible frameworks and libraries (cross-platform
meets standard JS), and I didn't trust myself to build them from scratch.

[^2]: On the contrary, I'm pretty sure I stick with bad strategies for too long.

[^3]: If you're like me, your idea of what you want is like a compass's
idea of north in the Bermuda Triangle, or a cat's idea of a straight line:
confused. But everyone knows what other people want.

[^4]: My evidence isn't all negative, either: I have worked on other people's
problems before, as an intern at Medium-sized Company A. I was successful and
learned a lot from it, even when the experience didn't agree with my dyspeptic
soul. I would have struggled even more than I have in the last 8 months
without the work I did at MCA on other people's problems. For example, one of
the only stories I managed to put together when I was writing was about workers
at a (medium-sized) company, and all of the code I wrote drew on skills I'd
learned while working at MCA.
